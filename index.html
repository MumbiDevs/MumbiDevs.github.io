Welcome to MAZEQUEST!

Jog your brain and get lost and found in our brain-challenging game.

This is MAZEQUEST, where every twist and turn challenges your mind and instincts! In this exciting 2D maze game, built with the powerful Godot Engine, you'll need sharp wits and a good sense of direction to navigate through intricate mazes. Along the way, you'll discover hidden treasures, dodge sneaky traps, and uncover secrets that only the bold can find. Think you have what it takes to make it through? Get ready for a thrilling adventure that will keep you on your toes!

The goal is simple: navigate from the maze entrance to the exit while picking up as many objects as possible along the way. Sounds easy, right? Well, get ready for some twists and turns! Each maze is procedurally generated, so no two runs are ever the same. The deeper you go, the harder it gets!

Completed Features in Maze Quest

Maze Generation and Dynamic Layouts

One of the core features completed in Maze Quest is the maze generation system. Players are presented with unique, hand-crafted mazes that vary in difficulty, encouraging exploration and problem-solving. The dynamic layouts ensure that no two mazes feel identical, which keeps the gameplay fresh and challenging. This feature gives players new mazes to solve as they progress, providing increasing complexity as they advance through the game.

Treasure Collection and Traps

The treasure collection system is a key gameplay mechanic where players must navigate through the maze to find and collect hidden treasures. However, scattered throughout the mazes are traps that the player must avoid. Stepping on a trap results in a setback, such as losing treasures or restarting from a previous point. This feature adds layers of strategy, forcing players to weigh risks and rewards as they explore.

Smooth Player Movement and Collision Detection

Player movement in Maze Quest is fluid and responsive, allowing for smooth navigation through the maze. This feature includes robust collision detection, ensuring that the player’s character accurately interacts with walls, treasures, and traps within the maze. Players can intuitively control their movements while avoiding obstacles, enhancing the overall user experience.

Technologies Used in Maze Quest and Why We Chose Them
  • Godot Game Engine

    • We chose the Godot Engine as the core framework for building Maze Quest. Godot offers an intuitive interface and a powerful set of tools for 2D game development, making it the ideal choice for this project. It allows for rapid prototyping, and its node-based system made it easy to structure our mazes, traps, and player mechanics. Additionally, Godot’s scripting language, GDScript, is highly flexible and similar to Python, which made it easier to iterate quickly during development. Another reason for choosing Godot was its lightweight design and open-source nature, meaning we weren’t tied down by heavy licensing fees or bloated tools. The engine’s strong support for both 2D and 3D game development also provides future flexibility, should we decide to expand Maze Quest

  • GDScript

    • We opted to use GDScript for all the core game logic. GDScript is tightly integrated with Godot, allowing us to manage player controls, maze generation, and interactions with traps and treasures seamlessly. It’s easy to pick up, readable, and efficient for small projects like Maze Quest. The ability to directly interact with the engine’s scene and node structure through GDScript was a major reason for choosing it, as it made scripting gameplay mechanics straightforward.

  • Godot’s Animation System

    • For player movement and interactions, we utilized Godot’s animation system. This system made it easy to implement smooth transitions, whether it was the player moving through the maze or triggering traps. Its simplicity allowed us to focus on core gameplay without getting bogged down by overly complex animation pipelines.

  • Tilemaps

    • To design the mazes in Maze Quest, we relied on Godot’s built-in Tilemap system. This feature allowed us to easily create complex mazes by placing predefined tiles that represent walls, pathways, traps, and treasures. Tilemaps offer the flexibility we needed for creating dynamic mazes without writing extensive code for manual level design, saving us development time while still offering the ability to create visually distinct and varied mazes.

    Difficult technical challenges I encountered

    One of the most difficult technical challenges I encountered while developing Maze Quest involved implementing dynamic collision detection between the player, the walls of the maze, and in-game objects like treasures and traps. Early in development, I realized that without accurate collision detection, the player could walk through walls, miss traps entirely, and collect treasures without actually interacting with them. This issue undermined the entire gameplay experience, as it made navigating the maze trivial and broke the core mechanics of the game.

    The task was to design a system that could reliably detect when the player collided with a wall or object, providing immediate feedback such as stopping the player's movement or triggering a trap. To make this work, I needed to leverage Godot’s physics engine and its collision detection capabilities, but I hadn’t worked much with Godot’s Tilemap system before, which added complexity. The maze walls were drawn using tiles, and I had to configure the collision shapes of each tile correctly, making sure that player interactions would register smoothly.

    After some research, I mapped out how to use Godot’s built-in collision nodes, attaching them to the player and tiles. I tested the system, but I quickly ran into performance issues. The player would either get stuck when they shouldn’t or pass through walls, especially at the corners of tiles where collisions seemed inconsistent.

    To address this, I implemented a more efficient approach. I fine-tuned the collision shapes and reduced the precision to make calculations simpler while keeping the interactions accurate. I also added raycasting to detect player proximity to traps and treasures. After multiple iterations and debugging, the system finally worked as intended. The result was smooth, reliable player movement that interacted with maze elements as expected, which improved the game’s overall playability dramatically. This challenge taught me the importance of balancing accuracy and performance in collision detection, especially in puzzle-based games like Maze Quest.

    Technical Takeaways

    One key takeaway was the importance of understanding the underlying systems within a game engine. Initially, I tried to use Godot’s collision system without fully grasping how it works under the hood, leading to unnecessary frustration and wasted time. Once I took a step back to learn about the physics engine, tilemap integration, and how the engine handles collision shapes, I was able to solve the problem much more effectively. This experience reinforced the idea that taking time to fully understand the tools I’m working with often saves more time in the long run than rushing through implementation.

    What I Might Do Differently

    Looking back, I would have approached the problem with a more methodical plan from the start. I often dive into coding as soon as I have a rough idea of what I want to achieve, but this experience showed me the value of mapping out potential challenges and researching solutions beforehand. In future projects, I will invest more time in planning and prototyping before jumping into the implementation phase.
